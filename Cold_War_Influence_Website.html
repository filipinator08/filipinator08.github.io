Introduction to Cold War Influence

The Cold War was a conflict of ideologies: democracy and capitalism (U.S.) versus communism (U.S.S.R.). The U.S. focused on containing communism (e.g., Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan), while the U.S.S.R. aimed to spread communist influence. Both relied on indirect strategies: proxy wars, foreign aid, covert operations, and propaganda.

The Korean War (1950–1953)

North Korea (supported by the U.S.S.R. and China) invaded South Korea (backed by the U.S. and UN). The war ended in a stalemate but solidified Cold War divisions in Asia.

Primary Source: Truman's Press Release

Angolan Civil War (1975–2002)

The U.S.S.R. supported the Marxist MPLA government; the U.S. and South Africa backed UNITA rebels. Angola's conflict became a Cold War battleground in Africa.

Podcast: Cold War Conversations – Angola

Primary Source: CIA Declassified Documents

Sandinistas vs. Contras – Nicaragua (1980s)

The leftist Sandinista government (backed by Cuba and U.S.S.R.) was opposed by U.S.-funded Contras. The Iran-Contra scandal revealed illegal U.S. arms deals.

Video: PBS Frontline: Nicaragua

Primary Source: Reagan Doctrine

Propaganda & Cultural Influence

Both nations used media, films, education, and cultural diplomacy to spread ideology. The U.S. ran Radio Free Europe, while the U.S.S.R. used state-controlled press and conferences.
